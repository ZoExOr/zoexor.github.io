Inside the Investments of Warren Buffett: Twenty Cases

Yefei Lu


I finished this book on a high-speed train. To be honest, a lot of the finance-heavy details were outside my comfort zone, and I don’t pretend to fully grasp every technical argument. What stayed with me, though, was the way the book framed decision-making – especially in the final chapters – which felt surprisingly relevant to where I am right now.

Stripped of the financial jargon, Buffett’s approach to investing is really about making the best possible decisions with imperfect information. The edge doesn’t come from clever tricks, but from a disciplined way of thinking.

One idea that stood out is the obsession with high-quality information. Not everything that looks like information actually is. What matters is data that is grounded, verifiable, and useful over time. Before acting, you do the homework properly; once you’ve done it, you stop chasing more opinions and focus on execution. Over time, the goal is not to know a little about everything, but to go deeper into a few selected areas so that past insights compound instead of being wasted.

Another recurring theme is sustainability and long-term relevance. Value is not about short-term performance or noise, but about what can grow and remain meaningful over time. I like thinking about this as paying attention only to things that genuinely reduce uncertainty or expand future possibilities — and learning to ignore the rest.

The book also emphasizes patience. When a real opportunity appears, you take it seriously. When it doesn’t, you don’t lower your standards just to stay busy. Waiting is part of the strategy.

Finally, there’s a strong focus on people. Great investments depend on great management — people with integrity, good judgment, and the ability to allocate resources wisely. Taken outside of investing, this feels equally true in life: people with real potential tend to be self-directed, honest, and deliberate about how they use their time.

I wouldn’t call this a finance book I “mastered.” But it was a useful reminder that clear thinking, patience, and respect for information are transferable skills — whether you’re investing, building things, or figuring out what direction to take next.
